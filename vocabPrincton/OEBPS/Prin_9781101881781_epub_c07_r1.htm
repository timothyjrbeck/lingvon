Chapter 7

Foreign Words

This chapter is designed to increase your familiarity with words or phrases that are commonly used in American culture that may not have originated from English words. The first definition listed is generally how the word is used in American speech.

APARTHEID (uh PART yt) n [Afrikaans] the policy of racial segregation and oppression in the Republic of South Africa

Though apartheid does refer to a specific policy in a certain place and time, it can be used more generally to mean any strict system of segregation and discrimination by race. The literal meaning in Afrikaans is “apartness.”

BLITZKRIEG (BLITS kreeg) n [German] a swift, overwhelming attack employing speed and surprise

The literal translation means lightning war, and in WWII, blitzkriegs referred to the massive air attacks that the Germans carried out. Today the word is often shortened to blitz.

CHAI (chye) n [Swahili and many South Asian dialects] tea

To Westerners, chai denotes particular kinds of spiced black tea, but in many places in Africa and India, it is the generic word for tea [so to say chai tea is actually redundant].

CHI (chee) n [Chinese] life force

Traditional Chinese philosophy holds that chi is inherent in everything, and a proper balance of positive and negative forms is essential for good physical health. You may also see this word spelled qi.

CHUTZPAH (HUTS puh) n [Yiddish] audacity, gall

Generally chutzpah connotes brashness, in a negative sense; however, it is often used admiringly in English speech.

COUP D’ÉTAT (KOO day TAH) n [French] a sudden, violent overthrow of the government

The literal meaning in French is blow of the state. You may already be familiar with the word coup on its own, and it’s often used to mean any victory; coup d’état is used more for governmental and organizational overthrows.

DOPPELGÄNGER (DAHP el gang ur) n [German] a wraith or ghostlike twin of a living person; an alter ego

German for double, doppelgänger usually refers to an alter ego, or haunted version of a living person.

FIESTA (fee EST uh) n [Spanish] party

Though we generally use the term to mean any party, in Spanish it often connotes a religious festival or large celebration.

GESTALT (geh SHTALT) n [German] shape or form

While there is no direct translation from German for gestalt, we generally use the term to mean anything having to do with shape, form, or arrangement.

INTIFADA (in ti FAH dah) n [Arabic] rebellion, uprising

While the generic meaning of intifada is the throwing off of oppression, it can also specifically refer to the Palestinian uprisings in the late 1980s.

KANBAN (KAHN bahn) n [Japanese] the means by which JIT [just-in-time] manufacturing is managed

The original meaning of kanban is sign or card, and it refers to a system of just-in-time (JIT) manufacturing created to minimize the need to store anything during the manufacturing process. It can also refer to the signaling system used to indicate that something is needed at each step in the process.

KUDOS (KOO dohz) n [Greek] praise, accolades

In Greek this word means that which is heard of, and we suppose that if your boss (finally!) gave you kudos for a job well done, you’d probably want the world to hear about it, too.

MAHATMA (mah HOT mah) n [Sanskrit] a title of respect for a holy or wise man

Perhaps the most familiar use of the word mahatma to Westerners is in reference to Mohandas Karamchand Gandhi, who is often referred to as Mahatma Gandhi out of respect.

MENSCH (mensh) n [Yiddish] a decent human being

Literally human in the German variants that influenced Yiddish, a mensch is a complimentary term that means someone who can always be counted on to do the right thing.

OMBUDSMAN (AHM buds mun) n [Swedish] one who is appointed to investigate complaints against organizations and public officials

Most universities have an ombudsman, though many students may not know what his or her official duties are. NGOs private companies, and other organizations may have them, as well.

PATOIS (pah TWAH) n [French] jargon or specialized language

The original meaning was clumsy or nonstandard speech, such as the provincial speech you might have that marked you as being from a certain region.

SCHADENFREUDE (SHAH den froy duh) n [German] pleasure derived from the misfortunes of others

A person might feel schadenfreude when she didn’t get a desired promotion, but the person who got the job is having an awful experience. This comes from the German Schaden (destruction) and Freude (joy).

SHALOM (shuh LOM) n [Hebrew] hello, good-bye

The literal meaning is peace, which has become a greeting in the English-speaking world, as well. Other languages have related words, such as the Arabic salam and city names such as Salem and Jerusalem share roots with this word, as well.

SIESTA (see EST uh) n [Spanish] nap

Though we tend to just use siesta to mean a nap, siesta in the Spanish tradition is a nap after the midday meal, and the word shares a root with the Spanish word for sixth, because the siesta would take place in the sixth hour or midday.

TAPAS (TAH pahs) n [Spanish] appetizers or small servings of food

Tapas are generally ordered and shared among a group, and can be anything from plates of olives to more elaborate or substantial side dishes.

Tapa is actually Spanish for cover, and the etymology of the term is under dispute. To add to the confusion, with the increasing popularity of tapas bars throughout the world, we’ve heard more than one conversation in which someone mistakenly thought someone else suggested going to a topless bar to eat.

TSCHOTCHKE (CHAHCH kee) n [Yiddish] inexpensive trinket

Originally from a Slavic word for toys, tschotchkes can be a variety of knickknacks, baubles, or souvenirs. The spelling on this word can vary significantly.

YOGA (YOH guh) n [Sanskrit] a type of physical exercise imported from India

In Sanskrit, yoga means union, and the physical exercise is meant to join the body with the mind, or with the divine.
